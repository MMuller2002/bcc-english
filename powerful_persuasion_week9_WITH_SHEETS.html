KC
Key concepts | ReferenceUnderstanding the Rhetorical Situation
Referencev

Before analysing any advocacy text, you need to understand the rhetorical situation -- who is speaking, to whom, and in what context. These concepts help you read First Nations advocacy texts with understanding:

ConceptWhat it means in this context
Ethos of lived experienceAuthority drawn from personal and community experience -- not credentials or titles. Often the most powerful form of ethos.
Positioning the audienceMany First Nations advocacy texts position the non-Indigenous Australian reader as both complicit in injustice AND capable of choosing differently. Watch for how this dual positioning works.
Historical context as argumentReferences to the Stolen Generation, land rights, the 1967 Referendum and the Apology are not just background -- they ARE the argument. You need the context to read the text.
Cultural language choicesTerms like Country, Community, Elders and mob carry meaning beyond their surface definitions. They signal cultural authority and frame the argument for a specific audience.
AIATSISThe Australian Institute of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Studies (aiatsis.gov.au) is the primary resource for contextual information about First Nations culture, history and advocacy. Your teacher may direct you there for additional resources.

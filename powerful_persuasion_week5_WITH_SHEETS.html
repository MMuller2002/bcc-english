Hook · 0–8 minThe Evidence Auction — Rate Strongest to Weakest
0–8 min▼

Five pieces of evidence. Rate each one — STRONGEST, GOOD, or WEAK — based on how credible and useful it would be in a persuasive speech.

"Studies show that phone-free schools improve learning."Source: Not given
"According to a 2023 OECD report, students in phone-free classrooms scored significantly higher on standardised international tests than peers in schools that permitted phone use."Source: OECD Education at a Glance, 2023
"My friend said their school banned phones and it was way better for everyone."Source: Personal anecdote (unnamed friend)

Rule of thumb: the more specific the source, the year, and the data — the stronger the evidence.

Key concepts · 8–20 minEvidence Quality & Speech-Ready Format
8–20 min▼

Copy the Evidence Quality Guide into your notes. Use it when evaluating every source you find today.

Evidence TypeQualityHow to use it
Peer-reviewed researchSTRONGEST"A study in [journal], [year], found that…"
Government / official reportSTRONG"According to the [body]'s [year] report…"
Expert opinion / professional bodySTRONG"The [association] states that…"
Quality journalism (ABC, The Conversation)GOOD"A [year] [outlet] investigation found…"
Personal anecdoteMODERATEUse for pathos — never as sole evidence.
Unsourced blog / social mediaAVOIDFind a primary source instead.
Speech-ready format — always record evidence this way"According to [source], [paraphrased evidence]. This demonstrates [link to argument]." — Never copy-paste. Always paraphrase. Always record the source.

Key concepts · 8–23 minVocabulary Banks & Words to Avoid
8–23 min▼

Copy the Vocabulary Banks (Worksheet 5C Part A). Then commit these two lists to memory — they will transform your speech.

Weasel words — AVOID

obviously, clearly, everyone knows, it's common knowledge, it's clear that, basically, sort of, kind of, pretty much, quite, really quite

These assert without proving. They tell the audience to agree before you've given them a reason to.

Red flag words — AVOID

absolutely everyone, always, never, complete disaster, literally, the worst, everyone agrees, total failure, obviously

These overstate the case. Audiences distrust speakers who use absolute language — it signals lack of nuance.

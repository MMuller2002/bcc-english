Key concepts · 8–23 minMedia Framing & the CRAAP Test
8–23 min▼

Media framing = the way a story is told shapes what we think about it. The same facts, presented differently, produce different emotional responses and different conclusions.

Word choice

Selecting words with specific connotations.

"Protesters" vs "activists" vs "rioters"

Inclusion / omission

Choosing which facts to include — and leave out.

Reporting the cost of a policy but not its benefits.

Headline emphasis

What's in the headline is what audiences remember.

"Crime rises" vs "Crime at decade low except one category"

Source selection

Choosing whose voice counts as expert or witness.

Only quoting critics — never supporters — of a policy.

Key concepts · 8–23 minVisual Literacy Vocabulary
8–23 min▼

Copy these into your Reference Card. Tap each card to reveal its persuasive effect — then write the effect in your notes.

Salience

The most visually prominent element — what draws the eye first.

Tap to reveal effect ↓ What the creator wants you to notice first shapes your first impression of the whole image.

Social Distance

How close or far subjects appear (close-up = intimacy; wide shot = distance).

Tap to reveal effect ↓ Close-ups create empathy and personal connection. Wide shots create objectivity, grandeur, or emotional detachment.

Gaze

Whether subjects look directly at the viewer (demand) or away (offer).

Tap to reveal effect ↓ Direct gaze creates a personal relationship — the subject "demands" your attention. Averted gaze invites you to observe without being noticed.

Vector

Lines, gestures, or sight-lines that direct the eye toward something.

Tap to reveal effect ↓ Vectors guide your attention to what the creator considers important — often a product, a message, or a person's face.

Colour Connotation

The emotional and cultural associations of specific colours.

Tap to reveal effect ↓ Red = urgency/danger/passion. Green = nature/growth/hope. Blue = trust/calm/authority. Black = power/death/mystery. Yellow = warning/energy/joy.

Symbolism

An image that represents something beyond its literal meaning.

Tap to reveal effect ↓ Symbols compress complex ideas into a single recognisable image. A dove = peace. A chain = oppression. A sunrise = hope or new beginning.

Framing

What is included or excluded from the image's edges.

Tap to reveal effect ↓ What is cropped out is just as meaningful as what is kept. Framing makes editorial choices about what context the audience sees — or doesn't see.

Apply it

An advertisement shows a child looking directly at the camera with a wide-eyed expression, with an adult partially visible behind her out of focus. Which visual technique is PRIMARILY being used to create empathy?

Key concepts · 10–25 minPerspective, Representation, Voice & Absence
10–25 min▼

Copy these four concepts into your notes. You will use all four in your analysis this lesson.

ConceptDefinitionQuestion to ask when reading
PerspectiveThe point of view from which a text is written — shaped by the author's position, experience, and purpose.Who is speaking? What is their relationship to this issue?
RepresentationHow people, places, groups, and issues are portrayed — the words and images chosen to describe them.How are different groups described? Are any dehumanised, celebrated, or marginalised?
VoiceWhose words and experiences are included in a text. Who is quoted, interviewed, and centred.Who gets to speak for themselves? Whose story is told by someone else?
AbsenceWhat — and who — is left out of a text, and the effect of that omission.Whose perspective is missing? What would change if we heard from them?
The ethics of voiceProverbs 18:17 — "In a lawsuit the first to speak seems right, until someone comes forward and cross-examines." Proverbs 31:8-9 — "Speak up for those who cannot speak for themselves." As future persuasive speakers, you have a responsibility to how you represent others — especially those who don't have the same platform.

Guided practice · 25–45 minTwo Texts, Two Worlds
25–45 min▼

Read both texts carefully. They address the same issue — social media age restrictions — from very different perspectives.

Text A — Parent advocacy group statement "The evidence is clear: social media is causing measurable harm to young people. Rates of anxiety, depression, and cyberbullying among teenagers have risen sharply in the years since social media became ubiquitous. As parents, we have a duty of care. We are calling on the federal government to enforce strict age verification — no social media access for under-16s. This is not about limiting freedom. It is about protecting children from an industry that has repeatedly shown it values profit over the wellbeing of young users."
Text B — Year 9 student op-ed "Once again, adults are making decisions about our lives without asking us what we think. Social media is how we maintain friendships, access support communities, share our creative work, and stay informed about issues that matter to us. Yes, there are problems — but the solution is not to lock us out entirely. That just drives the activity underground and removes the safeguards that exist on mainstream platforms. What we need is better education, better platform accountability, and — for once — someone to actually listen to young people instead of talking about us."

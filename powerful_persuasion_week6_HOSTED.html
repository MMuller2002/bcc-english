Key concepts | 8-20 minIntroduction Architecture: Four Functions
8-20 minV

Copy this structure into your notes. Every function must appear in your introduction. All four. Every time.

FunctionPurposeTarget lengthCommon mistake
HOOKGrab attention. Make the audience feel something or want to know something before you state your position.20-40 wordsStarting with "Today I will be talking about..." -- boring and wasted.
CONTEXTGive the audience enough background to understand why this issue matters now.30-50 wordsGiving too much history. You have 3-5 minutes total -- get to the point.
CONTENTIONState your position clearly and confidently. No hedging. No "I think maybe."15-25 words"I think this is quite an important issue." Not a contention -- a non-position.
PREVIEW (optional)Briefly signal your three arguments. Gives the audience a roadmap.15-25 wordsListing arguments in full -- save that for the body paragraphs.

Function identification

Which sentence is a CONTENTION? "I contend that social media platforms should be legally required to verify users are 16 or older."

Key concepts | 5-25 minSpoken PEEL Checklist + Live Modelling
5-25 minV

This checklist stays on the board for the whole lesson. Use it to check every paragraph you draft.

StepWhat it must doSpeech language
[P]Begins with a signpost word. One clear, confident sentence. States the argument directly."First..." / "Furthermore..." / "Finally..."
[E]Evidence paraphrased (not copied) and cited naturally in the sentence."According to [source], [finding]." / "A 2023 study found that..."
[Exp]2-3 sentences explaining HOW and WHY the evidence proves your point. This is where your argument lives."This demonstrates..." / "What this means is..." / "This matters because..."
[L]Returns to contention in one clear sentence. Can address the audience directly."This is why I contend..." / "For this reason, I urge you to..."
OverallRead aloud: 45-75 seconds per paragraph. No stumble points. No sentences you cannot say in one breath.Dashes and short sentences mark natural pauses.
Watch your teacher model-draft a paragraph liveListen to the decisions narrated aloud: why each signpost word was chosen, how evidence is paraphrased and cited in speech language, where the explanation step does the analytical work, and how the paragraph is shortened when a sentence causes a stumble.

Rebuttal workshop | 8-32 minDrafting Your Rebuttal
8-32 minV

The rebuttal demonstrates intellectual honesty and depth. State the opposing view fairly -- then dismantle it with evidence. Never strawman.

Rebuttal draft -- use the sentence frame below

"Some may argue that [opposing view -- state it fairly and charitably]. [Acknowledge what is legitimate about this concern.] However, [dismantle using evidence or logic]. The evidence demonstrates that [counter-claim supporting your position]."
Extension (GATEWAY)After drafting, find the single strongest piece of evidence that supports your rebuttal. Is it cited in your paragraph? Can you be more specific about the source and the finding?
🏁
Conclusion drafting | 32-52 minDrafting Your Conclusion
32-52 minV

The conclusion is not a summary. It is your final act of persuasion. Every element below must appear. Use the checklist as you write.

DO include:
  • ✓ Contention restated (not word-for-word)
  • ✓ 3 arguments synthesised (1 line each)
  • ✓ Echo the hook (refer back to your opening)
  • ✓ Call to action -- direct and personal
Do NOT include:
  • ✗ "In conclusion, I have talked about..."
  • ✗ New arguments introduced for the first time
  • ✗ Trailing off -- "So, yeah, that's my speech."
  • ✗ Apologising or thanking people mid-conclusion

Conclusion builder -- draft each element, then write it as one continuous piece

SupportUse this scaffold: "Today I have argued that [contention]. First, I showed you [arg 1]. Furthermore, the evidence confirmed [arg 2]. Finally, I demonstrated [arg 3]. [Echo hook]. [Call to action]."

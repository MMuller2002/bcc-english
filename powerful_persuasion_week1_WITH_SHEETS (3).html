Key concepts · 10–25 min The PAP Framework
10–25 min ▼

Copy this vocabulary into your glossary. You'll use these terms all unit.

TermDefinitionExample
PersuasionAny attempt to influence thinking, feeling or behaviour through communication.An ad, a speech, a petition.
ContentionThe main argument the author wants you to accept."Eating less meat saves the planet."
AudienceThe intended group the text is created for.Teenagers, parents, voters.
PurposeThe reason the text was created — to sell, inform, entertain, advocate.To sell a product.
PositionWhere the author stands — their point of view on the topic."Fast food is harmful to teenagers."

Quick check — PAP framework

A charity ad says: "Every 60 seconds a child goes hungry. You can change that."
What is the purpose of this text?

Key concepts · 10–25 min Fact, Opinion, Bias & Connotation
10–25 min ▼
CategoryDefinitionTip to identify
FACTA statement that can be tested and verified.Can I prove this? Is there data?
OPINIONA viewpoint or judgement — not verifiable.Look for evaluative words or hedging (I believe, it seems).
BIASOne-sided presentation using loaded language or selective facts.What's left out? What words carry extra weight?

Guided practice · 25–45 min Two-Source Analysis
25–45 min ▼

Read BOTH texts about the same event. Highlight opinion words in one colour and bias language in another.

"Dedicated local residents gathered peacefully at the Town Hall to voice their concerns. Families, seniors and community leaders united to protect the neighbourhood's character and green spaces."Source A — Community newsletter
"A small but vocal group of protesters attempted to derail the progressive development proposal. The project faces resistance from a minority opposed to change and growth."Source B — Property developer blog

1. List three words from each source with strong connotations. What attitude does each create?

2. Which source is more biased? Quote specific words in your justification.

3. What does each source leave out? What would a neutral account include?

Extension (GATEWAY)Write a third "neutral" version using only verifiable facts. Is true neutrality possible?
SupportLook for: adjectives that judge (dedicated, small), verbs that imply intent (attempted, derail), nouns that frame the group (residents vs protesters).

Key concepts · 8–28 min Three Powerful Devices
8–28 min ▼
Device 1

Emotive Language

Words deliberately chosen to trigger an emotional response: fear, sympathy, anger, hope, pride.

Example: "crisis" vs "problem" — both describe difficulty, but "crisis" triggers urgency and alarm.
Key insight: Every emotive word carries a hidden argument — it tells you how to feel before you've decided whether you agree.
Device 2

Allusion

An indirect reference to a well-known person, event, or text — borrowing authority or emotional weight from that source.

Example: "This is our David vs Goliath moment" — borrows the Biblical story of the underdog. The audience doesn't need an explanation; the story does the work.
Faith connectionProverbs 16:24 — "Gracious words are a honeycomb, sweet to the soul and healing to the bones." The metaphor of honey borrows from a shared understanding of sweetness and nourishment to describe the power of kind words.
Device 3

Metaphor

A comparison that says one thing IS another. Metaphors don't just describe — they frame. Once you accept the metaphor, you accept its logic.

Economy example: "The economy is a machine" implies it needs engineers and can be fixed. "The economy is a living organism" implies nurturing and recovery. Same topic — completely different argument built into each metaphor.
Political example: "We are fighting for the soul of this nation." — What does "fighting" imply? What does "soul" imply?

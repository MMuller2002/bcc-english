Speech A — Formal address (school principal to parent body) "The data is unambiguous. Research conducted across twelve OECD nations demonstrates that when young people participate in community decision-making processes, outcomes improve by measurable margins across health, education, and civic indicators. Yet in Australia, the median age of local councillors remains 52. This is not a coincidence — it is a structural failure. I call on every organisation in this room to establish a formal youth advisory mechanism within the next twelve months."
Speech B — Student speaker at a youth summit "Every single day, decisions get made about our schools, our streets, our future — and we are not in the room. We are told to wait. To be patient. To trust that the adults have it covered. But we have watched our planet warm, our housing become unaffordable, and our mental health services underfunded — while the decision-makers wait for us to grow up. We are not waiting anymore. We are here. And we are not going away."

Your initial reaction

Which speech would be more persuasive to you personally — and why?

Neither is objectively "better" — the answer depends entirely on the audience. That's today's core insight.

Key concepts · 8–23 minPEEL — In Depth
8–23 min▼

Copy this table into your notes. The Explanation step is where most students are weakest — and where marks are won or lost.

LetterStands forWhat it doesSpoken signal language
PPointStates the argument in one clear sentence. Always begins with a signpost word."First… / Furthermore… / Finally…"
EEvidenceSupports the point with data, examples, expert opinion, or case study."Research shows… / For example… / According to…"
EExplanationExplains HOW and WHY the evidence supports the point. This is the hardest — and most important — step."This demonstrates… / This matters because… / What this means is…"
LLinkReturns to the contention. Reinforces why this argument matters to the whole speech."This is why I contend… / Ultimately… / For this reason…"
The Explanation Step — why it mattersThink of it this way: the Evidence is a witness at a trial. The Explanation is the barrister asking: "So what does this mean? Why does this prove our case?" Without that step, the jury has to work it out themselves — and they might not reach your conclusion. The explanation is where YOUR argument lives.

Identify the missing PEEL element

"Furthermore, young people clearly have valuable ideas to contribute to politics. Their perspective is unique and often overlooked. For this reason, the voting age should be lowered to 16." — What is missing?

Key concepts · 8–26 minCohesive Devices — Three Types
8–26 min▼

Copy the Reference Card (Worksheet 4C Part A) as your teacher explains each type.

TypeFunctionExamples
Coordinating conjunctionsJoin clauses of equal weight (within a sentence)for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so — FANBOYS
Subordinating conjunctionsJoin a main clause to a dependent clausealthough, because, since, while, unless, whereas, if, until
Additive connectivesAdd information or a further pointfurthermore, moreover, in addition, also, additionally
Adversative connectivesShow contrast or concessionhowever, nevertheless, on the other hand, despite this, yet
Causal connectivesShow cause and effecttherefore, consequently, as a result, for this reason, thus
Temporal connectivesShow time sequencefirst, subsequently, finally, meanwhile, previously, since then
NominalisationTurns verb/adjective into a noun — condenses and elevates registerdecide → decision | fail → failure | significant → significance | develop → development
Why nominalisation matters"We failed to protect the environment. This was significant." → "Our failure to protect the environment had significant consequences." The nominalised version is tighter, more authoritative, and flows better in a speech. It also lets you refer back to a previous idea in a condensed form — which is exactly what high-quality academic prose does.

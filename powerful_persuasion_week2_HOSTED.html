Key concepts · 8–23 minThe Speech Blueprint
8–23 min▼

Think of a persuasive speech as a building. Every part has a purpose — and the structure is what makes it stand. Copy this blueprint into your notes.

SectionPurposeKey signposting language
HookGrab attention. Make the audience care about the issue before you've stated your position.A question, statistic, anecdote, or bold claim.
ContentionState your position clearly — no ambiguity."I contend…", "I argue…", "It is clear that…"
Arguments (×3)Build your case with evidence. Each is a PEEL paragraph."First…", "Furthermore…", "Finally…", "The evidence shows…"
RebuttalAcknowledge the opposing view — then dismantle it. Shows intellectual honesty."Some argue… however…", "While it may seem… in fact…"
ConclusionReinforce your contention. Issue a call to action. Return to the hook's emotion."In conclusion…", "The evidence is clear…", "I urge you to…"
Why the rebuttal mattersAddressing the opposing view doesn't weaken your argument — it strengthens it. Proverbs 18:17 says "In a lawsuit the first to speak seems right, until someone comes forward and cross-examines." A speaker who anticipates objections demonstrates integrity and depth of thinking.

Check your understanding

A student finishes their three arguments and then says: "Now I know some people think this is too expensive — but let me explain why that concern doesn't hold up." Which section is this?

Key concepts · 10–22 minThe Evaluation Sentence Stem
10–22 min▼

This is the most important analytical tool in your unit. Copy it into your notes now. You will use it in your assessment.

Evaluation Sentence Stem In the [section], the speaker uses [technique/device] to position the audience to feel/think [effect]. This is effective for [audience] because [reason], as it [elaboration].

Example In the introduction, Thunberg uses the rhetorical question "How dare you!" to position the audience to feel directly accused and morally responsible. This is effective for a world leaders audience because it collapses the distance between the speaker and the powerful — forcing them to confront the gap between their rhetoric and their action, rather than remaining comfortable observers.

Key concepts · 8–28 minFive Rhetorical Devices — Your Reference Card
8–28 min▼

Copy these definitions into your Reference Card (Worksheet 2C reverse side) — you'll use it for your own speech.

Device 1

Ethos

Appeals to credibility and trustworthiness. Why should the audience believe the speaker?

"As a scientist with 30 years of research in this field, I have seen the data firsthand."
Effect: Builds trust. Establishes authority. Makes the audience think: "This person knows what they're talking about."
Device 2

Pathos

Appeals to emotion. Targets the heart, not the head.

"Think of the children in our community who go to sleep hungry tonight."
Effect: Creates empathy, urgency, moral responsibility. The audience feels — and feeling precedes action.
Device 3

Logos

Appeals to logic and reason. Data, facts, evidence, and sound argument.

"Studies show a 40% reduction in accidents when speed limits were lowered by 10 km/h."
Effect: Demonstrates the argument is rational and researched. Satisfies the sceptical audience member.
Device 4

Tricolon

A list of three parallel phrases or clauses. Creates rhythm and makes ideas memorable.

"We came, we saw, we conquered." / "Invest in people, invest in future, invest in hope."
Effect: Three feels complete. The rhythm sticks in the listener's memory long after the speech ends.
Device 5

Rhetorical Question

A question asked for effect — no answer expected. Positions the audience as someone who already knows.

"How dare you?" / "Do we really want to leave this legacy for our children?"
Effect: Creates urgency, implicates the audience, and invites agreement without demanding it.
Rhetorical devices in scripturePaul is one of history's greatest rhetoricians. In 1 Corinthians 13:1 he uses pathos powerfully, and in Acts 17 he deploys ethos and logos in his address to the Areopagus. These aren't tricks — they're tools for communicating truth effectively. How we speak matters as much as what we say.
